Beluru Shri Chennakeshava Swamy Temple

The Belur Shri Chennakeshava Swamy Temple, located in Karnataka, is a masterpiece of Hoysala architecture dating back to the 12th century. Dedicated to Lord Chennakeshava (Vishnu), it is renowned for its intricate stone carvings depicting gods, goddesses, mythical beasts, and scenes from Hindu epics.

The temple's main sanctum houses a beautifully adorned statue of Lord Chennakeshava, accompanied by sculptures of other deities like Venugopala and Lakshmi. Its exterior and interior are adorned with intricate sculptures and decorative motifs, showcasing exceptional craftsmanship and artistic skills.

Visitors can explore the temple complex, admire the detailed carvings on pillars and walls, and experience the serene atmosphere of this historic religious site. The Belur Temple is not only a significant pilgrimage site but also a cultural treasure that attracts art and history enthusiasts from around the world, offering a glimpse into India's rich architectural and religious heritage.

Manjarabad Fort

Manjarabad Fort, situated near Sakleshpur in Karnataka, is a remarkable example of 18th-century military architecture commissioned by Tipu Sultan in 1792. Its distinct star-shaped design, a rarity in India, was strategically planned to offer panoramic views of the Western Ghats and to provide effective defense against potential invaders. The fort's layout consists of eight walls radiating from a central point, each angled to enable multiple firing positions.

During the Anglo-Mysore Wars, Manjarabad Fort played a crucial role as a strategic outpost, guarding the route between the coastal and inland regions. Its strategic significance is evident in Tipu Sultan's vision to secure his kingdom's boundaries and maintain control over the surrounding territories.

Today, the fort is a popular tourist attraction, offering visitors a glimpse into Karnataka's historical past and Tipu Sultan's military prowess. Tourists can explore the fort's well-preserved structures, including ramparts, bastions, and underground tunnels, while enjoying breathtaking views of the lush Western Ghats. Accessible from Sakleshpur, Bangalore, and Mangalore, the fort is best visited during the cooler months from October to March, providing an ideal climate for exploring its architectural marvels and appreciating the scenic beauty of its surroundings.

Bettada Byraveshwara Temple

The Bettada Byraveshwara Temple, located in Hassan district, Karnataka, is a serene Hindu temple dedicated to Lord Byraveshwara, a form of Lord Shiva. Nestled amidst the scenic Western Ghats, the temple is renowned for its tranquil atmosphere and panoramic views.

Built in the Dravidian architectural style, the temple features intricate carvings and sculptures depicting various Hindu deities and mythological figures. Its elevated position on the Bettada Betta hill offers visitors stunning vistas of lush greenery and rolling hills.

The temple attracts pilgrims throughout the year, with a significant annual fair held in February drawing devotees from across Karnataka. The festivities include traditional rituals, prayers, and cultural performances, adding vibrancy to the spiritual experience.

Surrounded by dense forests, the temple also appeals to nature enthusiasts and trekkers seeking to explore the scenic beauty of the Western Ghats. Accessible from Hassan and Sakleshpur, the temple is best visited during the cooler months from October to March for a pleasant and enriching pilgrimage or day trip experience.

Bhagawan Bahubali Statue (Gommateshwara)

The Bhagawan Bahubali Statue, also known as Gommateshwara, stands as an awe-inspiring symbol of Jain philosophy and craftsmanship in Shravanabelagola, Karnataka. Carved from a single block of granite in 981 AD during the reign of the Ganga dynasty, this monumental statue rises to a height of 57 feet (17 meters) atop Vindhyagiri Hill.

Symbolizing the Jain ideals of renunciation, self-control, and non-violence, the statue depicts Lord Bahubali in a standing posture, with detailed carvings of ornaments and symbols adorning its surface. The craftsmanship and dedication of the artisans from centuries past are evident in every intricately carved detail, reflecting the rich cultural heritage of India.

Shravanabelagola attracts pilgrims and tourists from across the globe, especially during the Mahamastakabhisheka festival held once every 12 years. During this auspicious event, the statue is bathed in a series of elaborate rituals involving offerings of milk, saffron paste, and other sacred substances.

Accessible via a climb of approximately 600 steps leading to the hilltop, visitors not only witness the majestic statue up close but also enjoy panoramic views of the surrounding countryside. The spiritual aura and historical significance of the Bhagawan Bahubali Statue make it a profound and memorable destination for all who visit.
